# Termination Block

Insert this block into Section 8 of contract-structure.md

---

## 8. Termination

### 8.1 Termination for Convenience

Either party may terminate this agreement with fourteen (14) calendar days written notice.

### 8.2 Payment Upon Termination

If Client terminates, Client is responsible for:
- Full payment for all completed work (delivered milestones)
- Payment for work-in-progress, prorated based on completion percentage
- Reimbursement for any third-party costs incurred (hosting, tools, etc.)

Service Provider will transfer all deliverables completed to date within five (5) business days of final payment.

### 8.3 Work in Progress

Upon termination, Service Provider will:
- Deliver all completed deliverables
- Provide documentation of work in progress
- Transfer all project files and code (once payment is received)
- Not charge for work beyond the proration date

### 8.4 Survival

The following sections survive termination:
- Intellectual Property (Section 3)
- Limitation of Liability (Section 5)
- Confidentiality (Section 6)

### 8.5 Termination for Cause

If either party materially breaches this agreement and does not cure within ten (10) business days of notice, the other party may terminate immediately.

### 8.6 Effect of Termination

Termination does not relieve Client of payment obligations for work already completed or in progress.

---

**Explanation:**

A termination clause protects both parties:

- **Client protection:** Can exit if things aren't working, with 14 days notice
- **Your protection:** Get paid for work done, even if they terminate
- **Clear process:** Both parties know what happens next

**Why This Matters:**

- **Flexibility:** Both parties can get out if the relationship isn't working
- **Payment protected:** You don't work for free
- **Professional:** Shows you're organized and prepared
- **Fair:** 14 days is standard notice period

---

**Typical Termination Scenarios:**

### Scenario 1: Client Gets Impatient
> "We don't need this anymore. Can we stop?"
>
> Response: "Yes. 14 days notice. You pay for everything we've completed, plus prorated for work in progress. I deliver what's done, you can hire someone else to continue."

### Scenario 2: You Get Overwhelmed
> "I need to step back from this project."
>
> Response: "14 days notice. I deliver everything completed, document the work in progress, client pays for what's done, can hire someone else to finish."

### Scenario 3: Scope Exploded
> "This project has expanded way beyond what we agreed."
>
> Response: "Scope creep is a termination trigger. Either we do a change order for expanded scope, or we terminate. With termination, you pay for work done."

---

**Variants:**

### No Termination for Convenience

> Neither party may terminate except for cause (material breach).

**Use only if:** You have a long-term retainer and want stability. Not recommended for projects.

### Longer Notice Period

> Either party may terminate with thirty (30) calendar days written notice.

**Use for:** Longer projects where more transition time is needed.

### Penalty for Early Termination

> If Client terminates before Phase 2 is complete, Client forfeits the Phase 2 milestone payment.

**Use carefully:** Can anger clients. Only use if you want to strongly discourage termination.

### Retainer Termination

> Either party may terminate with thirty (30) days notice. Final invoice includes work through the notice period.

**Use for:** Ongoing retainer work (different from project-based).

---

**Common Objections:**

**"Why do we have to pay for work-in-progress?"**

Response: "Because I've already spent time and resources on it. If you terminate, I'm stopped mid-work and those hours don't translate to value for me. Prorated payment is fair—you only pay for the percentage of work actually done."

**"14 days isn't enough notice."**

Response: "For a project timeline, 14 days is standard. It gives me time to wrap up, document everything, and transition the work. If you need longer, we can adjust—but that means extending the project timeline."

**"What if we pay upfront? Can we terminate without payment?"**

Response: "No. Whether you pay upfront or per milestone, you're responsible for work delivered. Upfront payment is an advance on deliverables. If you terminate, I deliver what's been completed, and you either accept it or we count it as work done."

---

**What You Must Have:**

1. ✅ 14-day notice period (minimum)
2. ✅ Payment for completed work
3. ✅ Prorated payment for work-in-progress
4. ✅ Delivery of deliverables upon payment
5. ✅ IP/Liability/Confidentiality survive termination

**Don't Agree To:**

- ❌ Termination without notice
- ❌ Termination without payment
- ❌ Free support during transition
- ❌ No payment for work in progress

---

**Payment Math Example:**

```
Total contract: $15,000 (3 phases @ $5k each)
Phase 1: Complete
Phase 2: 50% complete
Client terminates

Payment owed:
- Phase 1: $5,000 (complete)
- Phase 2 (50%): $2,500 (prorated)
- Phase 3: $0 (not started)
- Total: $7,500
```

If you had already received Phase 1 ($5k), they owe $2,500 more.
